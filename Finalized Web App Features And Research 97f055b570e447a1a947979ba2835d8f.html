Refugees: the Big Picture

  • Who is a refugee?
    • A refugee can be defines as someone who has been forced to flee their country due to persecution, war, or violence
    • A refugee may have fear of persecution for reasons such as reace, religion, nationality, political opinion or membership in a particular social group
    • They cannot return home or are afraid to do so
    • War and ethnic, tribal and religious violence, sexual orientation and gender violence are all of many leading causes of refugees fleeing their home countries

  • Statisics on Refugees in Canada (UNHCR)
    • Refugee Arrivals in Canada 1980-2017

      “Canada has welcomed 1,088,015 refugees since 1980. This number includes those who were recognized as refugees in Canada or who were resettled from overseas.”

    • Unemployment Rate

      “Refugees have almost the same unemployment rate as Canadians. They contribute to this country’s economy and are not a burden on Canadian taxpayers as few are unemployed. The unemployment rate for refugees aged 25 to 54 is 9%, close to that of Canadian-born citizens (6%). The situation for refugees improves as they spend more time in Canada. Refugees who arrived in Canada between 1981 and 1990 have an unemployment rate of 6%, identical to those born in Canada.”

    • Income

      “Refugees prosper and join Canada’s middle-class within five years of their arrival. Refugees who arrived as adults earn on average $20,000 in employment income in their first year. While this is less than half the Canadian average, their annual earnings climb steadily. Data from the 2014 tax year show that a significant proportion of refugees who have been in Canada for at least five years earn middle-class incomes. Nearly one in four refugees (23%) earned between $40,000 and $79,999 annually, similar to the percentage of Canadians (27%) and total immigrants (24%) earning a middle-class income.”

    • Paying Taxes

      “Canada’s investment in refugees pays off. Over time, refugees pay more in income tax on average than they receive in public benefits and services. However, this does not represent all taxes paid (like sales taxes) since it only includes income tax. Refugees increasingly narrow the gap between income tax paid and public benefits and services received the longer they live in Canadian provinces and territories.”

    • Skill Levels

      “Half of refugees (51%) working are employed in high-skilled jobs, which includes doctors, dentists, architects, service managers and software engineers. In 2016, 33% of refugees worked in jobs that required high school and/or job-specific training (e.g. truck drivers, food and beverage servers, industrial butchers). About one fifth of refugees were employed in professional jobs that required a university degree (e.g. doctors, dentists, architects).”

    • Entrepreneurship

      “Refugees create jobs for both themselves and other Canadians. Including those who are self-employed and those who own companies, 14.4% of refugees who have been in Canada between 10 and 30 years are entrepreneurs, compared to 12.3% of people born in Canada. Refugees use their diverse skillsets and talents to start businesses and create jobs for themselves and other Canadians.”

    • Aging Population

      “Canada has an aging population, with the average age increasing from 37.7 in 2001 to 41.0 in 2016. Refugees are on average 11.1 years younger than those born in Canada, which means they are more likely to be working age. The average age of a refugee who came to Canada in 2016 was 28.9 years old. Refugees often come to Canada early in their lives, with many years to contribute.”

    • Home Ownership

      “Home ownership is an indicator of a household’s financial health, as well as a family’s commitment to a community. Despite their initial lack of financial resources, 65 per cent of refugee families who have been in Canada for 10 years or more live in homes they own, compared with 79 per cent of Canadian-born citizens. About one-third of refugee families managed to buy their own homes within their first five years in the country.”

    • Citizenship

      “Refugees have the highest citizenship uptake rate of all immigration categories. To become Canadian citizens, refugees must live in the country for at least three years, pay a fee, and pass a test on their knowledge of Canadian history, geography, economy, government, laws and symbols. 89% of refugees become citizens compared to 84% of Economic Class immigrants and 80% of Family Class immigrants.”

  • How do Refugees Come to Canada?
    • Refugees must qualify for entry under Canada’s lwas and must pass medical and security checks before they can come to Canada
    • There are different eligibilities and qualifications that a refugee must pass in order to be resettled in Canada
      • These include whether the individual is a convention refugee or person in need of protection
        • These decisions are often made by the Immigrant and Refugee Board of Cnada (IRB)
    • More Information Here:
  • What types of Refugees does Canada Recognise?
    • Refugees arrive under one of four categories as defined below:
      • Government Assisted Refugee (GAR)
        • Refugees are referred to canada for resettlement by the UNHCR
          • Individuals cannot apply directly and must register for refugee status with the UNHCR or with state authorities
        • Entirely supported by Government of Canada or province
        • Support delivered by non-governmental agencies funded by immigration, refugees, and citizenship of Canada (IRCC)
          • Includes ISS of BC
        • GARs recieve support up to one year from date of arrival in Canada or until they can support themselves (whichever happens first)
      • Privately Sponsored Refugee (PSR)
        • A PSR is sposnored by a group of people in Canada committed to helping the individual adjust to life in Canada
          • Helps in finding a place to live, financial support, social and emotional support and food/clothing expenses
          • Supports the PSR for one year after arrival or until they can support themsleves (whichever happens first)
      • Blended Visa Office Referred (BVOR)
        • BVOR program matches refugees identified for resettlement by the UNHCR with private sponsores in canada
        • key features of this program include:
          • The UNHCR identifies the refugees;
          • The Government of Canada provides up to six months of income support;
          • Private sponsors are responsible for another six months of financial support, and up to a year of social and emotional support;
          • The Interim Federal Health (IFH) Program and provincial health care cover refugees for the length of the sponsorship (one year)
      • Refugee Claimant
        • A refugee claimant is an individual in need ofprotection in Canada who cannot return to their home country safely because they could be subject to;
          • Danger or torture
          • risk to their life
          • reisk of cruel an dunusual treatment or punishment
        • Canada offers refugee cprotection to these individuals who must make a claim to the Immigration and Refugee Board of Canada (IRB) to stay legally in Canada

Multicultural Meanings of Social Support among Immigrants and Refugees

  • Gap of understanding in of newcomers, refugees, and immigrants seeking refuge in canada, specifically in how they view meanings of social support and their support needs and resources
    • Here to Help BC definition of Social Support

      Social support is the physical and emotional comfort given to you by your family, friends, co-workers and others. It's the knowledge that you are part of a community of people who love and care for you, value you and think well of you.

    • Specific Study Information

      The purpose of this study was to understand the meanings of social support for immigrants and refugees in Canada, and to explore the types and adequacy of formal supports. Individual interviews were conducted with 60 service providers and policymakers initially (Phase 1), and 120 immigrants and refugees (60 Chinese, 60 Somali) in the second phase.

  • This investigation revealed many interrelated challenges facing refugees and immigrants such as:
    • language difficulties
    • inadequate information on services
    • poor health
    • racism, needs for retraining
    • rejection of foreign qualifications
    • unemployment
    • social isolation
    • social insecurity
    • dwindling social network
    • and family conflicts

Self-Determination Theory (SDT) Background Information

  • Main Sources of Information
  • The self determination theory (SDT) is a theory of motivation that ahs been applied in numerous life domains such as health, sport, education, and work
    • Motivation - energy directed at a goal - plays a major role in lifestyle choices and in an individuals ability to make sustained changes necessary to maintain health
  • Self-determination refers to a person's ability to make choices and manage their own life
    • Being self-determined means that you feel in greater control, as opposed to being non-self-determined, which can leave you feeling that your life is controlled by others
    • “Self-determination theory suggests that people can become self-determined when their needs for competence, connection, and autonomy are fulfilled”

Components of Self-Determination Theory:

  • Autonomy: People need to feel in control of their own behaviors and goals. This sense of being able to take direct action that will result in real change plays a major part in helping people feel self-determined.
  • Competence: People need to gain mastery of tasks and learn different skills. When people feel that they have the skills needed for success, they are more likely to take actions that will help them achieve their goals.
  • Connection or relatedness: People need to experience a sense of belonging and atachment to other people.

SDT as a Framework for Positive Integration of Refugees

  • Canada has a population of over 130,000 refugees, many of which have experienced significant hardhip
    • Consensus is growing among researchers that forcibly displaced persons and refugees have gone through potentially traumatic experiences that may challenge their wellbeing and health
  • The self determination theory (read above toggle for more info) proposes that autonomy, competence, and relatedness are basic universal needs that are required for integrated personality development and wellbeing
    • Studies and literature reviews have shown that the increased experiences of hardships and trauma among refugees compared to the general population may significantly affect all aspects of their wellbeing and self determination
      • “These basic needs are likely to be unsatisfied in the lives of many refugees as they have lost control of their lives, are unable to use their skills in a job or hobby, and have lost their social networks. Integration into society fulfills the needs of refugees.” - from my inquiry on health and culture
  • A paper titled “Self-Determination and Psychological Adaptation in Forcibly Displaced People” (Turan, İpekçi, Yılmaz) investigates the use and implementation of SDT in supporting positive integration among refugees
    • NOTES:
      • In the context of supporting positive integration of refugees in Canada, SDT suggests that interventions and policies that promote autonomy, competence, and relatedness among refugees can enhance their motivation and engagement in the process of integration. For example, providing refugees with access to education and job training programs can promote feelings of competence, while offering opportunities for social connections and community involvement can promote relatedness. Additionally, respecting refugees' autonomy by involving them in decision-making processes related to their integration can also support their self-determination.

      Overall, using SDT to support positive integration of refugees in Canada would involve creating an environment that promotes autonomy, competence, and relatedness and involving refugees in decision-making processes related to their integration.

Finalized list of features

Digital Resource Hub:

  • A list of physical resources, viewable on a map and toggleable based on type of resource (community centres, mosques, food banks, housing etc.)
  • Digital resources, such as the Government of Canada website
  • How will the app be more curated and easily usable by refugees?
    • Audio guides throughout the web app
    • Ability to change into different languages using google translate embed
    • Writing a step by step “guide”

Map embed

  • implemented using google maps or mapbox api
  • functions:
  • uses markers to locate physical services
    • when marker is clicked on, it will display basic information about the location, including opening hours, languages and services provided
    • filterable by category of service (drop down box)
    • colour-coded and different icons based on category (if one location belongs to multiple categories, it will be multicoloured)
    • these locations will be limited to Surrey, BC for now, but may be expanded to Metro Vancouver later on
    • included locations:
      • places of worship
      • food banks
      • community events
      • employment services
      • childcare
      • social services
      • learning English
      • clinics and law offices which accept refugees
  • interactive map, with scroll and zoom
  • map interlinks with digital resource hub and telemedicine/teletherapy

Audio/video guides

  • implemented using <audio> tag in html
  • functions:
    • helps the refugee navigate through the resettlement process, as well as through day-to-day tasks in Canada
      • sortable by category
      • in addition to the audio, a short description of the contents of each audio
      • resettlement process examples: contacting settlement workers, documentation and paperwork
      • orientation in Canada examples: using public transport, places to shop for necessities, writing a professional resume
    • can be uploaded by anyone and then reviewed by a certified organization (implemented later) to cover a wider variety of topics and languages
    • downloadable, so can be used offline

Telemedicine/teletherapy

  • implemented using a list of links, with link to map embed and possibly a video calling/voIP (voice over IP) API (for full version of teletherapy, implemented later)
  • functions:
    • helps refugees find therapists, lawyers and especially doctors who speak the same language as them using a list
      • sortable by category
        • walk-in, pediatrician, dentist, therapist etc.
        • accepts special health insurance for refugees, only accepts MSP etc.
        • language (native language, dialect of native language, other languages)
      • each entry will include the doctor’s name, profession, language, location name and address
      • when clicked on, may include more information or show location on map
    • a digital platform that connects refugees and professionals directly without in-person contact (implemented later)
      • includes a contacts system with names of professionals already contacted, contact info and status
      • uses chat, voice chat and video calling for remote service delivery
        • this can save time for the refugee and the doctor/professional, as there is no need to schedule an appointment/travel to a location
        • if there is no doctor who speaks the same language within a close distance, video calling can also be useful
      • will strictly only be for certified professionals which the refugee already has had a physical appointment with/referred to
